## 1. Purpose

This document sets out sample fairness and assessment principles to guide recruitment and candidate evaluation activities. It is intended to support consistency, defensibility, and good process discipline in hiring.

## 2. Scope

These principles apply to all stages of recruitment where candidates are screened, assessed, compared, shortlisted, interviewed, or selected.

## 3. Core Principles

### 3.1 Job-Related Criteria

Candidates should be assessed using criteria that are relevant to the role and the work to be performed.

### 3.2 Consistency

The same core criteria and assessment structure should be applied across candidates in the same competition unless a documented and justified adjustment is made.

### 3.3 Transparency of Criteria

Required qualifications, preferred qualifications, and disqualifying factors should be identified as clearly as possible before substantive assessment begins.

### 3.4 Evidence-Based Assessment

Assessment conclusions should be based on evidence from resumes, interviews, assignments, references, or other approved methods rather than intuition alone.

### 3.5 Proportionate Assessment

The complexity and burden of the assessment process should be proportionate to the role.

### 3.6 Documentation

Important decisions and assessment outcomes should be documented sufficiently to support later explanation or review.

### 3.7 Respect for Candidates

Candidates should be treated respectfully throughout the process. Materials, timelines, and requests should be reasonable and relevant.

### 3.8 Confidentiality

Candidate information should be handled confidentially and shared only with those who need it for recruitment purposes.

## 4. Practical Assessment Rules

The following practical rules support fairer recruitment:

- distinguish required qualifications from preferred qualifications
- avoid vague or hidden criteria
- avoid changing the rules mid-process without clear documentation
- use common interview questions for comparable candidates
- capture rationale for shortlist and assessment decisions
- identify uncertainty explicitly
- avoid treating polished presentation alone as proof of suitability
- do not overvalue familiarity or personal similarity to the panel
- do not rely on one isolated data point where a fuller body of evidence exists

## 5. Caution Areas

The following situations require particular care:

### 5.1 Late Introduction of New Criteria
Adding new preferences after screening begins can undermine consistency and fairness.

### 5.2 Overreliance on Informal Impressions
Strong first impressions or interpersonal comfort should not override documented evidence.

### 5.3 Ambiguous Standards
Poorly defined criteria can lead to inconsistent application across candidates.

### 5.4 Insufficient Documentation
If rationale is not recorded, later review may be difficult even where the decision was reasonable.

### 5.5 Overreliance on AI or Automation
AI-generated summaries or recommendations may be helpful, but they should not be treated as self-validating or final.

## 6. AI-Supported Assessment Principles

Where AI is used in recruitment support:

- AI should support, not replace, accountable human judgment
- AI should be grounded in approved, job-related criteria
- AI outputs should identify assumptions and uncertainty
- AI outputs should be reviewed before use
- humans remain responsible for decisions and documentation

## 7. Interview Panel Expectations

Interview panel members should:

- review criteria in advance
- remain focused on evidence
- participate consistently
- document key observations
- avoid speculation
- support a respectful and professional process

## 8. Prototype Context Note

For demonstration or prototype purposes, these principles may be used as sample guidance to show how an HR-focused agent could be grounded in fairness-oriented recruitment practices.

They do not replace official policy, legal advice, or approved organizational standards.
